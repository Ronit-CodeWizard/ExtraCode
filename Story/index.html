Is That A Sign From God?
A Story of Faith, Love, and Destiny

Trying to shake off the excitement, I greeted some new faces and soon became friends with Shiv, Rohan, Gopal, and Prakash. Prakash was from Jadugoda, and the rest were from Turamdih‚Äîeach with their own stories, their own laughter.

By the sixth period, we had our library class. Curiosity got the better of me, so I asked Rohan who that girl was. He grinned and said, "Her name's Tanisha Singh. She's from Turamdih." Just hearing her name felt like unlocking a secret.

That evening, I asked Rohan for her Instagram handle. Her account was private, so I sent a follow request and waited. When she accepted‚Äîand even followed me back‚ÄîI was stunned. She didn't even know my name, and yet somehow, our paths had quietly crossed again.

She replied kindly, and from there, our chats grew little by little. I found myself trying to keep her attention, drawing her closer with every message.

I tried to move on, but when I learned that she'd stopped talking to him too, my heart betrayed me again. I began speaking to her once more.

I was paying attention, watching him explain a poem, when I suddenly noticed Tanisha glancing at me from behind her textbook. She was smiling faintly, trying to hide it from the teacher. When I turned to look at her, our eyes met. I raised my eyebrows slightly - silently asking, "What happened?" She smiled, gave a tiny "nothing" gesture with her expression, and quickly turned her face back to the board. It lasted only a few seconds, but it felt like an entire story written without words.

Months later, I heard they had broken up. A strange hope returned to me. I began talking to her again, noticing small hints that she still cared, though neither of us ever said it aloud.

When class 12 began, our batch had only fifteen students left‚Äîjust three boys: me, Dasmat, and Chaitan. We called ourselves the "Three Pillars of the Class." After the retests, Shiv and Rohan rejoined, and life started feeling normal again.

I knew she wasn't mine, and maybe never would be. So I tried to let go. I stopped messaging her, and she didn't reach out either.

I just sat there, stunned, smiling to myself. Maybe, just maybe, it really was a sign.
"God is still writing your story. Quit trying to steal the pen. Trust the author."
Maybe God was writing mine too.

After more than a month of this, she stopped texting me altogether. We even stopped talking in class. The silence between us grew heavy, like a wall neither of us could climb.

It stayed that way until the day of our half-yearly exam results. Our Physics teacher, B.K. Paul Sir, was checking our answer sheets in class. I was quietly watching how others had written their papers, curious to see where I stood. Then came the turn for Tanisha's paper.

When she noticed me glancing, she told me - in a friendly but serious tone - not to look. But curiosity got the better of me. I couldn't help but glance again, and I noticed she had scored low marks. She was already upset, and by looking at her paper, I made things worse. I hadn't meant to, but it still hurt her.

Later, Rohan came to me and said, "Bro, what were you doing? You really made her feel bad. Can't you see how upset she was when you were looking at her paper? It's like you cut your own line." His words hit hard. That night, I couldn't sleep. I kept replaying everything in my head - the look on her face, the guilt eating me alive.

I opened Instagram, typed a simple "Hey," wanting to say sorry‚Ä¶ but I couldn't bring myself to bring up the topic. I didn't want to remind her of what happened or ruin her night. So I deleted the message.

The next day, I planned to say sorry in person. But she wasn't at school. Two days later, while I was waiting in the corridor, I saw Angi, Rohan, Tanisha, and Anushka walking together. Anushka made some gesture with her eyes, and when I looked in that direction, Angi thought I was staring at Tanisha. She told Tanisha that I was looking at her and even said that I might be in love with her.

Tanisha already knew somewhere deep down that I had feelings for her. After that moment, I don't know what changed in her heart - but she stopped talking to me completely.

When I later asked Rohan what had happened, he told me everything. I realized there was no chance she'd speak to me again. One afternoon, Angi and Tanisha were sitting on the last bench, to my left. They were talking softly, when suddenly Angi looked at me and said, "You should participate in the solo singing competition." I was confused - then realized Tanisha had told her to say that. I wondered why Tanisha couldn't just tell me herself.

Out of nervousness or pride, I replied, "Why should I sing?" That was my mistake again - one of those small, stupid replies that carry big regrets later.

Even Rohan told me, "Bro, why are you breaking her heart like this? You talk nicely to everyone‚Ä¶ except her."
And honestly, I had no answer. I didn't know why I behaved that way - cold on the outside when all I wanted was to be close to her.

After that regret, the guilt inside me kept growing. I wanted to say sorry so many times, but for reasons even I couldn't understand, the words never came out. Once again, I stopped talking to her.

One day, a thought struck me ‚Äî what if I turned all this into a story?

Maybe writing would help me understand my own feelings. So I began writing everything down, page by page, trying to capture the moments I never had the courage to say aloud. I even showed my drafts to Shiv to get feedback.

After some time, they returned to the basketball area where I was sitting. Tanisha went to the staircase where the other girls were sitting. Angi went back to her dance practice team. Rohan looked at me and said, "Let's go to the Commerce and Arts building." I agreed.

I was feeling a bit hot, so I handed my blazer and my notebook to Tanisha before leaving, asking her to keep them safe until I came back. Fun fact: I had two notebooks that looked exactly the same ‚Äî one for rough work, and the other, my story-writing notebook.

I thought I had given her the rough one.
But as I walked away, I noticed Tanisha casually flipping through the pages. My heart dropped. That was my story notebook ‚Äî the one where I had written everything‚Ä¶ including her name.

I immediately said, "It's all useless, don't turn the pages. It's just rough work." A lie‚Ä¶ a desperate one. I just prayed she wouldn't read anything before I returned.

After somedays, I showed the notebook to Anushka for review, not realizing whether that was a good idea or a terrible one. She read it and instantly started teasing me. "Why don't you take reviews from the girl you're writing about?" she said with a smirk. Then, without warning, she called Tanisha over and handed her the notebook.

Tanisha came, took the notebook, and began reading. I still don't know how she read everything in just a few seconds ‚Äî or maybe she had already seen some parts earlier.

She looked at me and said, "Ohh‚Ä¶ this is for wifee, huh?"
I just smiled awkwardly and replied, "Maybe."

She wasn't angry. Instead, she told me gently, "You should change some lines‚Ä¶ make it easier to understand." I listened carefully to every suggestion she gave. Every word felt precious.

But then everything shifted again. Rohan later showed me his phone ‚Äî and on it were pictures of me and Tanisha together. I don't even know who took them or when. Suddenly, everything felt a little more real‚Ä¶ and a little more complicated.

After that, Group 1 had Physics practicals, so we went to the Physics lab. Group 2 had Chemistry practicals and went to the Chemistry lab. I was in Group 1. She was in Group 2.

Two different rooms.
Two different paths.
But the same story still silently tying us together.

After which we would talk to each other comfortably, and for a while, it felt like we could actually enjoy our moments together. The small conversations, the tiny smiles, the way she would look at me while pretending she wasn't ‚Äî everything felt warm and effortless again.

But just like always, the comfort didn't last forever. Slowly‚Ä¶ something began to change.

There were days when she seemed close, and days when she seemed distant. Days when she talked freely, and days when she barely looked in my direction. It felt like she was fighting something inside her ‚Äî maybe confusion, maybe hesitation, or maybe just fear of what people would say.

And I, without even realizing, started stepping back again. Not because I wanted to‚Ä¶ but because I was scared that if I went too close, I would lose her completely.
